<p><strong>Interviewer: </strong>Aun concediendo que Falange, como organización española, tenga estilo netamente español, etc., ¿a qué fascismo se aproxima más, al italiano o al alemán?</p>
<p> </p>
<p><strong>Rivera:</strong> Coincide con la preocupación esencial a uno y otro: la quiebra del régimen liberal capitalista y la urgencia de evitar que esta quiebra conduzca irremediablemente a la catástrofe comunista, de signo antioccidental y anticristiano. En la busca del medio para evitar esa catástrofe, Falange ha llegado a posiciones, doctrinales de viva originalidad; así, en lo nacional, concibe a España como unidad de destino, compatible con las variedades regionales, pero determinante de una política que, al tener por primer deber la conservación de esa unidad, se sobrepone a las opiniones de partidos y clases. En lo económico, Falange tiende al sindicalismo total; esto es, a que laplus valía de la producción quede enteramente en poder del Sindicato orgánico, vertical, de productores, al que su propia fuerza económica procuraría el crédito necesario para producir, sin necesidad de alquilarlo –caro– a la Banca. Quizá estas líneas económicas tengan más parecido con el programa alemán que con el italiano. Pero, en cambio, Falange no es ni puede ser racista.</p>
<p> </p>
<p><strong>Interviewer: </strong>Conceding that the Falange, as a Spanish organization, has a style that is fundamentally Spanish; to which fascism is Falangism closest to, Italian or German?</p>
<p> </p>
<p><strong>Rivera:</strong> Our primary objective coincides with both: the destruction of the liberal-capitalist regime while preventing that the destruction of said regime results in a communist catastrophe which is both anti-Western and anti-Christian. In the search of a middle ground to avoid this catastrophe, the Falange has arrived at positions and doctrine of vibrant originality; regarding the nation, we conceive Spain as a destined unity, compatible with internal diversity <span style="font-size:10px;">[this is regarding Catalonia, Andalucia, Euskarri, etc. - not the modern version of diversity</span>] but subservient to a political order whose primary focus would be the preservation of national unity, and which would be immune to the opinions of political parties and economic classes. Economically, the Falange professes total syndicalism; with the methods of production completely under the control of an organic vertical union of producers, whose own economic power would procure the credit necessary to produce, without the necessity to acquire it - expensively - from Finance. Perhaps economically the Falange has more in common with the German program than with the Italian. However, Falange is not and cannot be racist.</p>

<p>1930's Spain had no minorities and very little Jews, including a racist agenda in the Falange was pointless. Yet, for anyone well-read in Falange literature, the "Spanishness of Spain" as well as the exaltation of Western civilization is a noted and fundamental tenet of Rivera's Falangism. The Falange chose to focus on the preservation of the Spanish culture and national spirit, rather than focus on the destruction of others. This is very typical of Rivera's unique way of approaching politics. In today's world, where non-Whites directly threaten both Spain and Western civilization, the only way the Falange can meet the goals put forth by Rivera <em>is by having an explicitly racist stance.</em></p>
<p> </p>
<p>When dealing with the topic of race, one must look at what the Falange originally proposed to accomplish - what their ultimate goal was - rather than the course of action they proposed to take in the 1930's. 1930's Spain is very different from modern Spain. Today, Falangism in Spain can only be accomplished alongside a racial agenda, period. That often quoted sentence by Rivera doesn't reflect the complexity of the topic.</p>
